How I choose to switch to Clojure

  • I love Ruby, we are together for 10 years, and I'm glad with how it evolves etc. But there's two huge problems with it. The first one is 99% of contracts I had, were related to Ruby on Rails, and RoR use most of bad OOP practices. It's ok if you need to create small MVP fast. But after that, maintaining RoR app becomes a nightmare for the reasons above. There's a solution for the problem: avoid RoR and use nice tiny microframeworks instead and Object Mapper instead of Active Record (RoR's killer feature) anti-pattern. This approach I'm trying to sell to my clients, but almost nobody bought it. Too hard to find the client who understand what RoR is a bad basement for the big project
  • So recently I started to review all the languages I heard of or had experience with, looking for the one that will make me happy again. Most obvious choice was the Elixir, because it mimics Ruby and best parts of RoR in someway. But I decide to look further, looking for something completely different. The next one was Go, but Go looks too strange for me. I like the possibilities of Rust, how the code looks like, low-level nature etc. But Rust targets a bit different area than I'm focused at. Many years ago I heard about Clojure, the legendary "Simple made easy" was very inspiring, and I even tried to write something, using Emacs+evil mode+CIDER, but I was not ready yet to switch. For that time I already found amazing frontend Elm language, and I was really amazed in practice how immutability can reduce the pain I had with classical OOP approach. And only after the horrible war began, and the need of look for new job and new life arose, I decided that Clojure's time is come, finally. I'd like to change the way I think and work with its help
  • My current Clojure's practical experience is limited, but I'm angry to learn, and choosing what to re-implement in Clojure. One of the ideas is to partly port RoR's Spree e-commerce platform in pair with htmx js library for frontend. Anyway, I believe in rule "just build something!". Check my github soon :)

IT skills

  • APIs: They are the facade of your system, hiding its internal complexity. Everyone wants this facade to be nice for others, and I’m helping people with that
  • Testing: I have a weighted approach of testing. You shouldn’t overtest everything (at the beginning for sure), but you must be sure the critical parts work
  • Refactoring: This is huge topic, and only following best practices and carefulness lead to success. Sometimes this process takes a lot of time, even if you have detailed plan
  • Data parsing/normalizing: Probably my favourite types of tasks. I love data manipulations
  • Data scraping: Watching run logs of hundreds of simultaneous scraping connections really please my eyes
  • Tasks automation: As a programmer, you always have a mind/hands work to do. Automate repetitive tasks, but only after they become really annoying. Don’t automate too early
  • Networking: Every developer should have experience with building own “Anton”. Since the 2007, when I had two, and till now, when I have eight bare-metal servers in my house, I love to practice networking stuff
  • Clouds: As a freelancer I use clouds from the early AWS days, and my experience grow with each new client. There are huge amount of tools clouds provide, and I have been working with most popular of them. Sometimes building crazy things

Soft skills

  • Self-organization/time management: Being freelancer for the whole of my life, planning and time-management is a must to master
  • Analytical abilities: Learn your and others mistakes, track success, make future decisions based on both
  • KISS: Don't overcomplicate/overengineer, split complex tasks on subtasks, manage one at the time
  • Creativity: When you experienced with your tools, you can do amazing things with them
  • Perseverance: There's always a way to do the thing, but if stuck, try to find another solution with team
  • Problem Solving: Formulate the problem, that is 80% of solution. Consult with pros, use other's expertise and become better professional
  • Angry to learn/Curiosity: The more you know, the more ways you can do the task. Always learn something, even unrelated to your main focus. It's worth it
